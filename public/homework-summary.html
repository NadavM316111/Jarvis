Question: Describe the process of reunification in Italy during the 19th century. Reference Camillo de Cavour, Giuseppe Garibaldi, and Giuseppe Mazzini.

The reunification of Italy, known as the Risorgimento, was a complex process driven by three key figures with distinct roles. Giuseppe Mazzini was the ideological founder and visionary who inspired Italian nationalism through his writings and founded the revolutionary group "Young Italy" in 1831, advocating for a unified Italian republic based on democratic principles. Camillo di Cavour, serving as Prime Minister of Piedmont-Sardinia, was the pragmatic statesman who used diplomacy and military alliances, particularly with France in 1858, to defeat Austria and annex northern Italian states through strategic warfare and plebiscites. Giuseppe Garibaldi was the military hero who led his famous "Red Shirts" (Expedition of the Thousand) in 1860 to conquer the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies in southern Italy, which he then handed over to King Victor Emmanuel II, demonstrating his commitment to unification over personal power. Together, these three men - Mazzini providing the ideology, Cavour the diplomacy, and Garibaldi the military action - achieved Italian unification by 1861, with Rome finally joining in 1870. BONUS - Congress of Vienna: The Congress of Vienna actually HINDERED Italian unification by deliberately dividing the Italian peninsula into multiple states under Austrian dominance and restoring conservative monarchies. This "Metternich System" was designed to suppress nationalism and liberalism, placing Austrian Habsburgs directly in control of Lombardy-Venetia. However, this oppression ironically fueled Italian nationalist sentiment, uniting intellectuals and revolutionaries against foreign domination. The Congress had a similar impact on Germany, creating the German Confederation of 39 states under Austrian presidency. Thus, while the Congress of Vienna intended to prevent nationalism, it ultimately became a catalyst that intensified the desire for unification in both Italy and Germany.
