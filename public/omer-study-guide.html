Part 1: The Crusades & Medieval Jewish History

The Crusades (1096-1350s CE)
  • There were four major Crusades
  • Objective: Reclaim Jerusalem from Muslim control for Christianity
  • Recruited mainly German peasants with money and religious zeal

Impact on Jewish Communities

  • Jews were killed, tortured, and forced to convert
  • Homes and synagogues were destroyed
  • Communities were looted and dispossessed
  • Jews were blamed for Jesus's death (though the Gospel says Romans crucified him)
The Dark Ages & Church Control
  • Lasted approximately 1,000 years
  • Common people were kept illiterate by the Church
  • Could not read the Gospel to verify Church teachings
  • This enabled mass manipulation and control

Easter & Anti-Jewish Violence

  • Easter falls around the same time as Passover
  • Churches preached that Jews killed Jesus
  • Mobs would attack Jewish communities after Easter services
  • This connection explains some Omer mourning customs

Part 2: Rabbi Akiva & The Bar Kokhba Revolt

Historical Context (132-135 CE)
  • Jews lived under Roman rule from early 1st century BCE to ~500 CE
  • Temple destroyed in 70 CE by Romans
  • Two Jewish factions existed:
    • Group 1 (Judeans): Military opposition - fight Rome
    • Group 2 (Northern Jews): Diplomatic approach - don't provoke Rome
Key Figures
  • Rabbi Akiva - Head of the ideological movement supporting revolt
  • Bar Kokhba - Military leader of the revolt
  • Rabbi Akiva believed Bar Kokhba would be the redeemer

The Revolt's Outcome

  • Initially successful - briefly took Jerusalem
  • Emperor sent 40,000 troops to exterminate Judeans
  • 90% of the population was exterminated
  • First genocide in Jewish history
  • Rabbi Akiva was skinned alive (tortured to death)
Roman Restrictions on Jews
  • Jews banned from Jerusalem
  • No circumcision allowed
  • No Shabbat or holidays
  • Could not sanctify the month/calendar
  • Men could not wear tefillin publicly

Part 3: The "Students" of Rabbi Akiva - Reinterpreted

The Talmudic Account
  • Sources vary: 24,000 pairs (12,000 pairs) or 12,000 or even 300 students
  • Location: From Givat (near Haifa) to Antipatris (near Jerusalem)
  • Both locations were Roman military/port cities
  • Died between Passover and Shavuot

Stated Reason for Death

  • Talmud says: "They did not treat each other with respect"
  • Also: "They were stingy with Torah knowledge"
  • Died of "Askara" - traditionally translated as diphtheria
The Real Meaning - Historical Analysis
  • "Askara" comes from Latin word "sicae" = dagger/sword
  • "Sicarii" = Jewish assassins who used hidden daggers against Romans
  • "Students" = followers/soldiers in the revolt, not Torah students
  • They died by the sword (killed by Romans), not from disease
  • The Talmud disguised the true cause to protect Rabbi Akiva's image

The Deeper Message

  • When Jews don't get along and disrespect each other = weakness
  • Enemies take advantage of Jewish disunity
  • Example: Israel's internal conflict before October 7th
  • The Talmud's message: Unity is essential for survival
Important Note

NOWHERE in the entire Talmud does it say Jews commemorated their death with mourning customs during the Omer!

Part 4: The Omer from the Bible

Biblical Source
  • Found in Vayikra (Leviticus) Chapter 23
  • Contains all Biblical holidays: Shabbat, Pesach, Omer, Shavuot, Sukkot, Yom Kippur
  • Hanukkah and Purim are NOT in Torah (rabbinic/post-Biblical)

What is the Omer?

  • A measurement: ~2 quarts / 2/3 gallon / 2 liters
  • A sheaf of the first grain harvest (barley/wheat)
  • Counting period: 49 days (7 weeks x 7 days)
  • 50th day = Shavuot (means "weeks")
Purpose of the Omer
  • Practical: Support the Kohanim and Levites (they had no land)
  • Spiritual: Show gratitude to God for providing
  • Food was also a commodity for trade

The Agricultural Cycle

  • Pesach = Spring (abundance, harvest begins)
  • Shavuot = Early Summer (dry season begins)
  • Sukkot = Before Winter (rainy season)
Key Rules
  • "Shabbat after Pesach" = Second day of Pesach (holidays = "Shabbatot")
  • Cannot eat new grain (chadash) until offering is brought
  • The Omer was elevated symbolically to God
  • Counting the Omer is a mitzvah from Torah (still done today)
Israel as God's Gift
  • God gave the Land of Israel to Jews conditionally
  • Condition: Follow the law/covenant
  • Jewish claim to Israel = Divine gift, NOT just conquest
  • Quran mentions Allah gave land to Jews 3 times

Was Omer Originally Sad or Happy?

HAPPY/FESTIVE!

It was a celebration of abundance and gratitude. The "sad" customs developed later in medieval Europe.
