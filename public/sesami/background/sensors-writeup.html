Sensors Technical Writeup

Detailed analysis of sensor technologies for infant developmental monitoring

Sensor System Overview

Sesami employs a multi-modal sensing approach combining computer vision with optional wearable IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit) sensors. This dual-modality approach provides comprehensive movement tracking while accommodating different use cases and user preferences.

Primary: Computer Vision System

Camera-based pose estimation and movement analysis

The primary sensing modality uses a high-definition camera with infrared capability for 24/7 monitoring. On-device computer vision processes video in real-time to detect body poses and track movements.

Computer Vision Pipeline

  • Pose Estimation: MediaPipe BlazePose extracts 33 body keypoints at 30fps
  • Body Segmentation: Distinguishes infant from background and objects
  • Movement Classification: Custom CNN classifies movement patterns
  • Milestone Detection: LSTM network identifies developmental milestones

Performance Benchmark

In testing, our computer vision system achieved 87% accuracy in detecting 12 key motor milestones, with processing latency under 100ms on Jetson Nano hardware.

Secondary: Wearable IMU Sensors

9-axis inertial measurement for continuous tracking

Optional wearable sensors embedded in baby clothing provide continuous movement data even when camera-based tracking is unavailable (e.g., stroller, car seat, covered by blanket).

Sensor Fusion Architecture

When both camera and wearable sensors are active, Sesami fuses data from multiple sources to improve accuracy and fill gaps in coverage.

Sensor Fusion Architecture Diagram

Fusion Benefits

  • Gap filling: Wearable data fills in when baby is occluded from camera view
  • Confidence boosting: Correlated signals from both modalities increase detection confidence
  • Subtle movement detection: IMU captures micro-movements camera may miss
  • 24/7 coverage: Portable wearable extends monitoring beyond nursery

Key Sensor Research Findings

Academic Validation

Our sensor approach is validated by peer-reviewed research:

  • Nature Scientific Reports (2019): "Automatic Posture and Movement Tracking of Infants with Wearable Movement Sensors" - demonstrated SVM classifiers can identify infant postures from accelerometer/gyroscope data
  • PMC (2019): "Identification of Developmental Delay in Infants Using Wearable Sensors" - showed full-day movement recordings can identify at-risk infants
  • Nature Communications (2022): "Intelligent wearable allows out-of-the-lab tracking of developing motor abilities" - validated motor milestone tracking from lying supine through independent walking

Technical Challenges & Solutions

Challenge Impact Our Solution
Infant movement variability Hard to distinguish deliberate movement from reflexes Temporal pattern analysis over multiple seconds
Clothing/blanket occlusion Camera cannot see body under covers IMU wearable provides backup data stream
Low-light conditions Standard cameras fail at night IR illumination + IR-sensitive camera sensor
Edge computing limits Complex models too slow for real-time Optimized lightweight models (MobileNet-based)
Battery life (wearable) Frequent charging frustrates parents Low-power BLE, 7-day battery life target

Safety Considerations

  • Wearable size: Minimum 45mm diameter to prevent choking hazard (exceeds CPSC requirements)
  • Materials: Hypoallergenic, CPSIA-compliant, no small parts
  • RF exposure: BLE 5.0 operates at very low power (1mW) - far below safety thresholds
  • Temperature: Thermal management ensures sensor never exceeds 40C
  • Camera privacy: Physical privacy cover, on-device processing only
