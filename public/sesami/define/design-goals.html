Define Phase

Design Goals, Criteria & Constraints

The specific objectives, success metrics, and limitations that guide our design decisions.

Primary Design Goals

The core objectives that Sesami must achieve to be successful

Safety First

Create a wearable device that is completely safe for infants during sleep and play, with no choking hazards, skin irritation, or interference with natural movement patterns.

Success Metric: Zero safety incidents in 10,000+ hours of testing

Accurate Detection

Achieve clinical-grade accuracy in detecting gross motor milestones (rolling, crawling, walking) that matches or exceeds current pediatric screening tools.

Success Metric: 90%+ sensitivity and 85%+ specificity for milestone detection

Seamless Integration

Design a solution that fits naturally into parents' existing routines without requiring constant charging, manual logging, or technical expertise.

Success Metric: 7+ day battery life, < 5 minutes setup time

Anxiety Reduction

Transform developmental monitoring from a source of parental stress into a tool that provides reassurance and confidence through clear, actionable insights.

Success Metric: 40%+ reduction in self-reported parental anxiety scores

Accessible Pricing

Make developmental monitoring technology affordable for middle-class families, not just a luxury item for wealthy parents.

Success Metric: Retail price under $150, comparable to premium baby monitors

Clinical Utility

Generate data that pediatricians find valuable and actionable, enabling better-informed discussions during well-child visits.

Success Metric: 70%+ of pediatricians rate data as "useful" or "very useful"

Design Constraints

Limitations and boundaries we must work within

Technical Constraints

  • Battery technology limits energy density - must optimize power consumption
  • Bluetooth bandwidth limits real-time data streaming - need edge processing
  • Small form factor limits sensor options - must use micro IMU chips
  • Infant skin sensitivity restricts material choices - hypoallergenic only
  • WiFi connectivity unreliable - must work with Bluetooth only

Regulatory Constraints

  • Cannot market as a medical diagnostic device without FDA clearance
  • Must comply with CPSIA lead and phthalate limits for children's products
  • FCC certification required for wireless transmission
  • COPPA compliance for any data collected about children
  • Cannot claim to diagnose, treat, or prevent any condition

Business Constraints

  • Initial development budget capped at $75,000
  • Must achieve positive unit economics at launch
  • Timeline: MVP ready for user testing within 6 months
  • Team size limited to 4 core members plus advisors
  • Must differentiate from Owlet, Nanit, and other competitors

User Constraints

  • Parents have limited technical expertise - must be plug-and-play
  • Sleep-deprived users - interface must be simple, no complex setup
  • Multiple caregivers - must support shared access (parents, grandparents)
  • Varying home layouts - must work in apartments and large houses
  • Users expect instant gratification - insights must be timely

Key Design Tradeoffs

Balancing competing priorities in our design decisions

More Sensors
Better accuracy, more data points
VS
Simplicity
Easier to use, lower cost, better battery
Decision: Single ankle sensor with high-quality IMU - sufficient for gross motor tracking without complexity
Real-Time Streaming
Instant insights, continuous monitoring
VS
Battery Life
Less frequent charging, better UX
Decision: Edge processing with periodic sync - 7 day battery with 4x daily data uploads
Medical Device Path
Clinical credibility, insurance coverage
VS
Consumer Product
Faster to market, lower regulatory burden
Decision: Launch as consumer wellness device, pursue FDA clearance as Phase 2 expansion
Subscription Model
Recurring revenue, lower upfront cost
VS
One-Time Purchase
Simpler for users, no ongoing commitment
Decision: Hybrid model - basic features included, premium AI insights as optional subscription
