Define Phase

End User Interviews

In-depth conversations with parents and caregivers to understand their experiences, needs, and pain points.

Interview Methodology

We conducted 45-minute semi-structured interviews with parents of infants aged 0-24 months. Interviews were conducted via video call and in-person, covering topics including daily routines, developmental concerns, interactions with healthcare providers, and technology usage. All interviews were recorded with consent and transcribed for thematic analysis.

JR

Jennifer R.

First-Time Mother
34 years old | Marketing Director | 7-month-old daughter | Miami, FL
Q: Walk me through a typical day with your baby. When do developmental concerns come to mind?
It's constant, honestly. I wake up and immediately check if she's breathing - I know it's irrational but I can't help it. Then during playtime, I'm always watching. Is she reaching for things enough? Why isn't she rolling over as much as my sister's baby did? I have this app where I log everything, but I forget half the time and then I feel guilty. The worst is at 3 AM when I can't sleep and I'm Googling 'when should baby sit up' for the hundredth time.
Q: How do you currently track your daughter's development?
I have the Wonder Weeks app, which tells me about mental leaps, and I use Baby Tracker for feeding and sleep. But for actual milestones? I have a baby book that I write in maybe once a month when I remember. My pediatrician gave me these CDC milestone checklists, but they're so vague. 'Does baby respond to sounds?' Well, sometimes? It depends? I never feel confident answering those questions.
Q: Tell me about your last pediatrician visit. How did the developmental assessment go?
It was 15 minutes total, including the shots. Dr. Martinez asked if she was rolling over and I said 'sometimes' and she said 'great, that's normal.' But I wanted to know - is it ENOUGH? Is she rolling the right way? She does this asymmetrical thing where she only goes one direction. I didn't even get to ask because we were already talking about the next vaccine. I left feeling like I still don't know if she's okay.

Key Insight

Parents want quantified, objective data - not just checkboxes. The vagueness of current milestone tracking ("sometimes," "enough") creates anxiety rather than reducing it. They need specific metrics they can point to.

DM

David M.

Father of Twins
41 years old | Software Engineer | 11-month-old twin boys | Fort Lauderdale, FL
Q: With twins, how do you approach developmental tracking?
It's chaos, to be honest. They're so different - Lucas is more physical, he was crawling at 8 months. Ethan is more verbal, he babbles constantly but he just started army crawling last week. Everyone keeps telling us 'twins develop differently' but that doesn't help when you're lying awake wondering if Ethan's delay is normal twin variation or something we should be worried about. We've basically given up on tracking apps because who has time to log for two babies?
Q: What would an ideal developmental monitoring solution look like for you?
Something that just... works in the background. I'm an engineer, I understand data. I want graphs, trends, comparisons. Not to other babies necessarily, but to milestones. Show me where Lucas and Ethan are on the curve. Tell me if Ethan's crawling delay is within normal range or if we should get an evaluation. And for God's sake, don't make me log anything manually. I have zero bandwidth for that.
Q: How would you feel about your babies wearing a sensor?
If it's safe? Sign me up. We already have them in sleep sacks every night, they wear socks all the time. If something could just passively collect data while they're doing their thing, that would be incredible. My main concerns would be: is it comfortable, can they choke on it, and does the battery last more than a day because I cannot manage another device that needs constant charging.

Key Insight

Time-strapped parents need passive, automated tracking. Manual logging is unsustainable, especially for multiples. The solution must integrate into existing routines (like sock-wearing) without adding burden.

SK

Dr. Sarah K.

Pediatrician & Mother
39 years old | Pediatrician (10 years) | 14-month-old son | Boca Raton, FL
Q: As both a pediatrician and a parent, what's your perspective on current developmental screening?
Professionally, I know the limitations. The ASQ-3 we use has decent sensitivity, maybe 75-80%, but that means we're missing 1 in 5 kids who actually have delays. And the specificity issue - we over-refer a lot of kids who turn out to be fine, which wastes resources and causes unnecessary parent anxiety. As a mom though, I'm just as neurotic as my patients' parents. I catch myself analyzing my son's gait, wondering if his W-sitting is a concern. Medical knowledge doesn't eliminate the worry.
Q: What data would you find most valuable to see from patients between visits?
Movement patterns, absolutely. So many developmental concerns show up in motor development first - muscle tone issues, coordination problems, asymmetries. If I could see objective data on how a baby is moving over weeks or months, that would be transformative. Right now I get a 5-minute snapshot in an exam room where the baby is usually crying or clingy. It's not representative. I want to see them in their natural environment.
Q: What concerns would you have about wearable tracking technology?
Safety is paramount - anything on an infant needs to be impossible to become a choking hazard. I'd also worry about parent anxiety. If you give parents raw data, they'll obsess over every dip and spike. The interface needs to translate data into actionable insights, not just numbers. And please, no false alarms at 2 AM. That's the last thing sleep-deprived parents need.

Key Insight

Even medical professionals experience parental anxiety. The solution must bridge clinical utility and parent-friendly communication - providing objective data that's interpreted into calm, actionable insights rather than raw metrics that fuel worry.

Emergent Themes

Patterns that appeared across multiple interviews

The 3 AM Google Spiral

Parents frequently described late-night anxiety sessions searching for developmental information online, often making their worries worse rather than better.

Mentioned by 14 of 18 participants (78%)

Pediatrician Time Crunch

Frustration with the brevity of well-child visits and feeling unable to get detailed answers about developmental concerns.

Mentioned by 16 of 18 participants (89%)

Comparison Trap

Constant comparison to other babies (siblings, friends' children, social media) driving anxiety even when pediatricians say development is normal.

Mentioned by 15 of 18 participants (83%)

Manual Logging Fatigue

Existing baby apps require too much manual input; parents start strong but abandon logging within weeks due to time constraints.

Mentioned by 13 of 18 participants (72%)

Desire for Objectivity

Strong preference for data-driven insights over subjective assessments. Parents want numbers and trends, not just "looks fine."

Mentioned by 17 of 18 participants (94%)

Safety as Non-Negotiable

Any wearable device must pass the "can my baby choke on it" and "will it irritate their skin" tests before parents will consider it.

Mentioned by 18 of 18 participants (100%)
