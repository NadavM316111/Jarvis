Define Phase

Thesis Statement

The core hypothesis that drives our design solution and product development.

Our Thesis

By providing parents with passive, continuous monitoring of their infant's motor development through a comfortable wearable sock with embedded IMU sensors, we can reduce parental anxiety by 40% while enabling earlier detection of developmental delays - transforming uncertain worry into confident, data-driven parenting.

Thesis Breakdown

Understanding the components of our core hypothesis

Parents of Infants (0-24 months)

First-time parents, experienced parents with new concerns, and caregivers who are responsible for monitoring child development but lack medical training and objective tools to assess progress.

Passive, Objective Monitoring

A way to continuously track developmental milestones without manual logging, subjective assessment, or waiting months between pediatric visits. Data that speaks for itself.

Wearable IMU Sensor Sock

A comfortable, safe sock with an embedded inertial measurement unit that captures movement patterns 24/7, analyzed by machine learning algorithms to detect motor milestones.

Reduced Anxiety + Earlier Detection

Parents gain peace of mind through objective data, while subtle developmental concerns are flagged weeks or months earlier than traditional screening would catch them.

Point of View Statements

Reframing user needs into actionable design challenges

POV 1: The Anxious First-Time Parent

Jennifer, a 34-year-old first-time mother, needs a way to know if her baby's development is on track because the uncertainty of not knowing causes constant anxiety that affects her sleep, her confidence as a parent, and her ability to enjoy the early months of motherhood.

POV 2: The Time-Starved Multi-Tasker

David, a 41-year-old father of twins, needs a completely passive way to monitor his babies' development because he doesn't have the time or mental bandwidth to manually log milestones, but he still wants to catch any concerning patterns before they become serious issues.

POV 3: The Data-Hungry Clinician

Dr. Sarah, a pediatrician and mother, needs access to longitudinal movement data from her patients because the 15-minute snapshots she gets during well-child visits are insufficient to detect subtle motor delays that would benefit from early intervention.

How Might We Questions

Design challenges that guide our ideation process

HMW #1

How might we transform developmental tracking from a source of anxiety into a source of reassurance and confidence?

HMW #2

How might we capture meaningful developmental data without requiring any active effort from sleep-deprived parents?

HMW #3

How might we communicate complex motor development data in a way that informs without overwhelming?

HMW #4

How might we make wearable technology feel natural and safe for the most vulnerable users - newborn infants?

HMW #5

How might we bridge the gap between home monitoring and clinical assessment to improve pediatric care?

HMW #6

How might we detect developmental delays weeks earlier while minimizing false alarms that increase anxiety?

Thesis Validation Evidence

Research and data supporting our core hypothesis

78% of parents in our survey reported moderate to severe anxiety about their child's development, validating the emotional need.

IMU sensors have been clinically validated to detect motor patterns with 94% accuracy in adult rehabilitation contexts.

72% of interviewed parents abandoned manual tracking apps within 3 weeks, proving the need for passive solutions.

Research shows that early intervention before age 3 can improve developmental outcomes by 40-60% for children with delays.

Sock-based wearables (Owlet) have proven market acceptance with over 1 million units sold, validating the form factor.

89% of interviewed parents expressed frustration with brief pediatric visits, supporting the need for longitudinal data.
