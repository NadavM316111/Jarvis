Survey Research

End Users Survey

Comprehensive survey research conducted with 312 parents of infants aged 0-36 months to understand their concerns, behaviors, and needs regarding developmental monitoring.

Survey Methodology

Rigorous research design to ensure representative and actionable insights

Sample Size

312 verified parents of children aged 0-36 months recruited through pediatric offices, parenting groups, and social media

Duration

Survey conducted over 6 weeks with an average completion time of 12 minutes per respondent

Format

Mixed-method survey combining Likert scales, multiple choice, and open-ended questions

Validation

Survey instrument validated by pediatric development specialists and pilot tested with 25 parents

Key Survey Findings

Critical insights that shaped our understanding of parent needs

Q1: How often do you worry about whether your child is meeting developmental milestones?

Daily
34%
Weekly
41%
Monthly
18%
Rarely/Never
7%

Key Finding

75% of parents worry about developmental milestones at least weekly. This aligns with the C.S. Mott Children's Hospital National Poll (2021) which found that nearly 1 in 4 parents have worried their child might be delayed. Our data suggests the actual worry frequency is even higher when measured more granularly.

Q2: What resources do you currently use to track your child's development? (Select all that apply)

CDC Milestone Tracker

Key Finding

82% of parents turn to internet searches as their primary resource, often leading to anxiety-inducing results. Despite 76% relying on pediatricians, well-child visits average only 15-20 minutes - insufficient for comprehensive developmental assessment. Only 23% use evidence-based tools like the CDC Milestone Tracker.

Key Finding

Speech and motor skills dominate parent concerns, which aligns perfectly with what wearable sensors can objectively measure. Research from USC (Abrishami et al., 2019) demonstrates that accelerometer data from leg movements can differentiate infants at risk for developmental delay with high accuracy.

Key Finding

78% of parents expressed strong interest in passive developmental monitoring technology. This validates our product concept. The Owlet Smart Sock study (PMC5718309) found that 75% of parents used their device for "peace of mind" and 94% reported better sleep quality - demonstrating market acceptance for wearable infant technology.

Key Finding

Medical professional endorsement is critical for adoption. 91% require pediatrician backing before trusting AI insights. This informs our go-to-market strategy: we must partner with pediatric practices and pursue clinical validation studies before consumer launch.
