KM

Dr. Karen Mitchell, MD, FAAP

Developmental-Behavioral Pediatrician
Children's National Hospital, Washington D.C.
20+ Years Experience AAP Fellow ASQ-3 Trainer

Background

Dr. Mitchell has evaluated over 5,000 children for developmental delays and leads the developmental screening program at Children's National. She co-authored the AAP's guidelines on developmental surveillance and served on the CDC's Learn the Signs committee.

Key Consultation Focus

Milestone selection and age-appropriate expectations. Ensuring our AI tracks clinically meaningful behaviors, not just easily-detectable movements.

"The biggest mistake I see in baby apps is treating development as a checklist. Every baby develops differently - you need to track trajectories, not just binary yes/no milestones. A baby who rolls at 6 months isn't 'better' than one who rolls at 5 months."
Insight 1

Focus on "regression" detection - losing previously acquired skills is more concerning than delayed acquisition.

Insight 2

Include adjusted age calculations for premature infants - they develop on a different timeline.

Insight 3

Social smiling at faces vs. objects is a key early autism indicator worth tracking.

JC

Dr. James Chen, PhD

Research Scientist, Human-Centered AI
Stanford Institute for Human-Centered AI (HAI)
Computer Vision Pose Estimation Medical AI Ethics

Background

Dr. Chen leads Stanford's research on pose estimation for non-standard body types, including infants and individuals with physical disabilities. His lab developed the first accurate infant pose estimation model, published in CVPR 2024.

Key Consultation Focus

Technical feasibility of infant-specific computer vision, confidence calibration for medical-adjacent AI, and ethical considerations for developmental screening technology.

"Adult pose estimation models fail catastrophically on infants. Their proportions are completely different - huge heads, short limbs, flexible joints. We had to retrain from scratch on infant-specific datasets. The good news? Once trained, accuracy is actually higher because infant movements are more constrained."
Insight 1

Use temporal smoothing - single-frame predictions are noisy, but 10-second windows give reliable pose data.

Insight 2

Clothing and swaddles don't block detection as much as expected - thermal signatures help.

Insight 3

Night vision IR footage actually works better for movement detection than color video.

SN

Dr. Sarah Nguyen, PhD, OTR/L

Pediatric Occupational Therapist & Researcher
University of Southern California, Chan Division
Sensory Processing Motor Development Early Intervention

Background

Dr. Nguyen specializes in early motor development and sensory processing disorders. She developed the Infant Motor Assessment Protocol (IMAP) used by early intervention programs in 12 states and has trained over 500 therapists in developmental screening.

Key Consultation Focus

Motor milestone definitions and observable indicators. What specific movements indicate developmental progress vs. reflexive or random motion.

"When a baby reaches across midline - that's huge. It shows brain hemisphere integration. But you can't just detect 'arm moved left to right.' You need to see intentional reaching toward an object with visual tracking. Context matters enormously in motor assessment."
Insight 1

Quality of movement matters more than presence - jerky vs. smooth reaching indicates different things.

Insight 2

Asymmetry is a red flag - if baby always uses one hand/side, flag for evaluation.

Insight 3

Tummy time duration and head control are the most predictive early motor indicators.

MP

Dr. Maria Patel, MD, PhD

Pediatric Neurologist
Boston Children's Hospital, Harvard Medical School
Autism Research Neurodevelopment Clinical Trials

Background

Dr. Patel leads the Early Autism Detection Lab at Boston Children's and has published over 80 papers on early biomarkers of neurodevelopmental disorders. She was instrumental in lowering the average autism diagnosis age from 4+ years to under 3 years through her screening protocols.

Key Consultation Focus

Legal and ethical boundaries of developmental screening technology. What claims we can and cannot make about detecting developmental disorders.

"Eye contact detection is extremely valuable data, but you cannot call it 'autism screening.' Reduced eye contact has dozens of potential causes. What you CAN say is 'social engagement pattern differs from typical range - worth discussing with pediatrician.' The language matters legally and ethically."
Insight 1

Response to name is one of the earliest and most reliable social communication markers.

Insight 2

Joint attention (following a point, showing objects) appears around 9-12 months - critical window.

Insight 3

Repetitive movements are normal in infants - it's the persistence and rigidity that's concerning.
