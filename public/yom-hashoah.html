THE HOLOCAUST

The Rise of Nazi Germany

In 1933, Adolf Hitler rose to power in Germany. Anti-Jewish laws were enacted, Jewish businesses were boycotted, and the systematic persecution of Jews began. The Nuremberg Laws of 1935 stripped Jews of their citizenship and basic rights.

Kristallnacht - Night of Broken Glass

On November 9-10, 1938, Nazi forces and civilians destroyed Jewish synagogues, businesses, and homes across Germany and Austria. Over 30,000 Jewish men were arrested and sent to concentration camps. This marked the beginning of systematic violence.

The Ghettos

Jews were forced into overcrowded ghettos across occupied Europe. The Warsaw Ghetto held over 400,000 people in an area of just 1.3 square miles. Starvation, disease, and death were constant companions for those trapped inside.

The Death Camps

Auschwitz-Birkenau, Treblinka, Sobibor, and other extermination camps became factories of death. Over 1.1 million people were murdered at Auschwitz alone. Gas chambers operated around the clock as the Nazis implemented their "Final Solution."

Liberation

As Allied forces advanced in 1945, they liberated the concentration camps and discovered the full horror of Nazi atrocities. Survivors, barely alive, bore witness to the systematic murder of six million Jews - men, women, and children.

Justice and Remembrance

The Nuremberg Trials held Nazi leaders accountable for crimes against humanity. Israel declared Yom HaShoah as Holocaust Remembrance Day. We remember not for vengeance, but so that such evil may never rise again.

TIMELINE OF TERROR

1933

Hitler Becomes Chancellor

The Nazi Party seizes power. Dachau, the first concentration camp, opens. Book burnings target Jewish authors.

1935

Nuremberg Laws

Jews are stripped of German citizenship. Marriage between Jews and non-Jews is forbidden. Systematic exclusion begins.

1938

Kristallnacht

The Night of Broken Glass. 267 synagogues destroyed. 7,500 Jewish businesses demolished. 30,000 Jews arrested.

1941

The Final Solution

Mass shootings begin in Eastern Europe. Einsatzgruppen murder over 1 million Jews. Gas vans are introduced.

1942

Wannsee Conference

Nazi officials coordinate the "Final Solution to the Jewish Question." Death camps begin systematic extermination.

1943

Warsaw Ghetto Uprising

Jews fight back against deportation to Treblinka. The uprising is crushed, but becomes a symbol of Jewish resistance.

1945

Liberation

Allied forces liberate the camps. The world witnesses the horror. Six million Jews - two-thirds of European Jewry - have been murdered.
