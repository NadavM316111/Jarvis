Chapter Summaries

Chapter 1: Summer 1935

Introduces Antonina Zabinska and the Warsaw Zoo. She has an extraordinary ability to connect with animals, sensing their emotions and needs. The zoo is a thriving place where she lives with her husband Jan, the zoo director.

Chapter 2: 1935-1939

Details the zoo's operations and the Zabinskis' daily life. Jan is a skilled zoologist focused on breeding programs. Their son Ryszard (Rys) is born. The zoo becomes one of Europe's finest, with rare species and scientific importance.

Chapter 3: September 1939

Germany invades Poland. The Luftwaffe bombs Warsaw, including the zoo. Many animals are killed or escape. Antonina must make heartbreaking decisions about which animals to save. The chapter captures the terror and chaos of the invasion.

Chapter 4: Fall 1939

Nazi zoologist Lutz Heck arrives to confiscate the zoo's most valuable animals for his breeding programs in Germany. He's obsessed with "back-breeding" extinct species like the aurochs. The Zabinskis must cooperate while hiding their true feelings.

Chapter 5: Winter 1939-1940

The occupation tightens. Jews are forced to wear armbands and face increasing restrictions. Jan begins to see opportunities to resist. The empty zoo cages become symbols of loss and potential.

Chapter 6: 1940

Jan proposes converting the zoo into a pig farm to supply the German army - secretly, this gives him an excuse to enter the Warsaw Ghetto to collect food scraps. This is the beginning of their resistance work.

Chapter 7: The Ghetto

The Warsaw Ghetto is established. Over 400,000 Jews are crammed into a small area. Jan witnesses the horrific conditions firsthand during his "pig farm" visits. The Zabinskis begin planning how to help.

Major Themes

Human-Animal Connection

Antonina's deep empathy with animals mirrors her later compassion for human refugees. The book suggests that kindness to animals and humans stems from the same source.

Nazi Ideology & Science

Lutz Heck's "back-breeding" program to recreate extinct "pure" species parallels Nazi racial ideology. Both seek to return to an imagined "pure" past.

Moral Courage

The Zabinskis risk everything to help others. Their courage develops gradually - they don't start as heroes but become them through choices.

Home & Sanctuary

The zoo transforms from animal sanctuary to human sanctuary. The concept of "home" and "safety" becomes central as the war destroys both.

Know: Date of invasion (Sept 1, 1939), what happened to the zoo animals, Antonina's emotional response, specific animals mentioned (elephants, etc.)

2. Lutz Heck's Role HIGH PRIORITY

3. The Pig Farm Plan HIGH PRIORITY

Know: Why Jan proposed it (excuse to enter ghetto), how it worked (collecting food scraps), what it enabled (smuggling people/supplies), the irony of helping Nazis while resisting them

4. Antonina's Character MEDIUM PRIORITY

Know: Her special ability with animals, her role as diary keeper (source of the book), how her empathy with animals foreshadows her later actions

5. Warsaw Ghetto Conditions HIGH PRIORITY

Know: When established (1940), population (~400,000), conditions Jan witnessed, why Jews couldn't easily escape, the yellow star/armband requirement

6. Symbolism of the Zoo MEDIUM PRIORITY

Know: Empty cages as symbols of loss, the zoo as sanctuary (first for animals, later for humans), the parallel between caged animals and trapped Jews

7. Historical Context MEDIUM PRIORITY

Know: Germany invaded Poland Sept 1, 1939 starting WWII, the occupation structure, how Poland was treated vs other countries

Potential Short Response Topics

Analyze the connection between Nazi animal breeding programs and Nazi racial ideology.

Key points: Both seek "purity," Heck's aurochs program mirrors Aryan ideology, irony of caring about animal genetics while committing genocide

How does Antonina's relationship with animals prepare her for her later role in the resistance?

Key points: Empathy, reading non-verbal cues, providing sanctuary, nurturing instinct, seeing worth in all creatures

Explain how the pig farm plan demonstrates the Zabinskis' moral courage and strategic thinking.

Key points: Using Nazi system against itself, creating legitimate cover, gradual escalation of resistance, personal risk

Describe the transformation of the Warsaw Zoo from chapters 1-7.

Key points: From thriving zoo to bombed ruins to pig farm, from animal sanctuary to potential human sanctuary, loss of innocence
