The Zookeeper's Wife

by Diane Ackerman

Study Guide: Chapters 16-23

Chapter Summaries

Chapter 16: Smuggling Lonia

▼

Jan attempts to smuggle Lonia Tenenbaum (Dr. Tenenbaum's widow) out of the Warsaw Ghetto. This chapter emphasizes the constant danger and unpredictability of rescue operations.

When Jan and Lonia reach the gate, the usual guard is not on duty - his wife is there instead. Jan boldly yells at the wife, intimidating her into opening the gate and letting them through. This demonstrates Jan's quick thinking and willingness to take risks.

Lonia stays at the zoo as a "Guest" for a couple of weeks before being moved to safer lodgings in the countryside. She leaves behind her dog, Zarka, at the zoo.

Key Points:

  • Jan's daring rescue of Lonia Tenenbaum from the Ghetto
  • The chapter opens with a comparison of the Zabinskis' activities to animal survival tactics - "ploy and counterploy"
  • The concept of deception as a survival mechanism (like chameleons and lion-fish)
  • Lonia becomes a temporary "Guest" at the zoo villa
  • The dog Zarka remains at the zoo after Lonia leaves

Chapter 17: Nazi Doctrine

▼

This chapter provides important context by explaining Nazi ideology and its implementation. Ackerman draws a powerful parallel: everything the Nazis did to the zoo - killing its plants and animals and replacing them with German ones - they began doing to the entire world.

The chapter serves as a thematic bridge, showing how Nazi "purification" ideology extended from nature to humanity, foreshadowing the atrocities to come.

Key Points:

  • Nazi ideology of "purification" and replacement
  • Parallel between treatment of zoo animals and treatment of humans
  • The zoo as a microcosm for Nazi occupation policies
  • Contextualizes the larger historical tragedy unfolding

Chapter 18: Seasons Change, Guests Return

▼

The year 1941 progresses through summer into autumn and then winter. During winter, "all the Guests were spirited away to other winter safe houses" for safety.

Keeping Antonina and Rys company are a rabbit named Wicek (Vincent in English) and a chicken named Kuba (Jacob). The mischievous rabbit "terrorizes the house like a real-life Bugs Bunny."

In Spring 1942, Guests begin arriving at the zoo once more. One significant arrival is Magdalena Gross, Antonina's close friend (BFF) from earlier in the book. Magdalena had tried to avoid staying at the zoo for as long as possible - not because she disliked it, but because she wanted to maintain some semblance of normal life. Eventually, the impossibility of normal life in Nazi-occupied Warsaw forces her to flee to the zoo.

Key Points:

  • Seasonal rhythm of hiding Guests - winter dispersal, spring return
  • Animal companions: Wicek the rabbit and Kuba the chicken
  • Magdalena Gross arrives as a Guest (Spring 1942)
  • Theme of trying to maintain normalcy in abnormal times
  • The impossibility of normal Jewish life under Nazi occupation

Chapter 19: Daily Life and the Warning Signal

▼

This chapter provides intimate details of daily life at the villa. Antonina kneads dough in the kitchen because the Guests need bread. Magdalena helps Antonina make croissants, joking: "I may not be able to sculpt in clay now, but I can still sculpt in flour!"

The Piano Signal: Whenever the Guests need to hide (because of unexpected visitors or danger), Antonina plays a specific song on the piano: Offenbach's "Go, go, go to Crete!" This serves as a warning signal meaning "go, go, go into hiding."

Magdalena wonders if she will ever be able to enjoy this music again - the song has become forever associated with fear and danger.

Key Points:

  • Daily routines: baking bread, making croissants
  • Magdalena's quote about sculpting in flour (her artistic spirit persists)
  • Critical: Offenbach's "Go, go, go to Crete!" = warning signal to hide
  • Music becomes associated with fear and survival
  • The psychological toll on the Guests

Chapter 20: Antonina's Illness & The Orphans

▼

In Autumn 1942, Antonina becomes stricken with a sickness that leaves her legs inflamed. She must stay in bed. The author speculates this may have been related to Antonina's pregnancy.

The chapter then jumps back to June 1942, when the Polish Underground receives a letter warning them of a mass execution of Jews. Antonina fears the Ghetto will be liquidated and worries about the zoo's safety.

Henryk Goldszmit's Story: The narrative jumps back to 1940 when a pediatrician named Henryk Goldszmit starts an orphanage. Then, in August 1942, Goldszmit and all the orphans are put on a train to Treblinka (a death camp). In 1971, Russians name an asteroid after Goldszmit in his honor.

Key Points:

  • Antonina's illness and possible pregnancy (Autumn 1942)
  • Warning of mass Jewish executions (June 1942)
  • Henryk Goldszmit (Janusz Korczak) - ran orphanage, sent to Treblinka
  • The tragedy of the orphans sent to death camps
  • Non-linear timeline showing interconnected tragedies
  • Asteroid named after Goldszmit (1971)

Chapter 21: Zegota and the Fox Man

▼

In Autumn 1942, a new Underground group called Zegota is formed. This organization was dedicated to "rescue, not sabotage or fighting" - it was the only organization of its kind in occupied Europe during the war. As part of Zegota, Jan and Antonina help save many Jews, and their zoo overflows with tenants.

The Fox Man: Continuing the strategy of hiding people in plain sight, the zoo becomes home to a Nazi-approved fur farm. In charge of it is Witold Wroblewski, a Pole nicknamed "the Fox Man." He raises foxes and other animals for fur, assisted by his cat Balbina (who will "never be anyone's fur coat"). Wroblewski uses Balbina as a wet nurse to feed young foxes.

Antonina starts caring for a large muskrat named Szczurcio. She puts it in a cage on the porch with a little swimming pool in a glass and watches it bathe daily. One day the muskrat escapes and is found in the chimney. In a lighter moment, after soup is spilled on the Fox Man's head, he lets birds eat noodles from his hair.

Key Points:

  • Zegota - Underground rescue organization (only one in occupied Europe)
  • Focus on rescue rather than sabotage or fighting
  • Nazi fur farm as cover - hiding in plain sight
  • Witold Wroblewski "The Fox Man" and his cat Balbina
  • Szczurcio the muskrat - another animal companion
  • Moments of humor amid tragedy (noodles in hair)

Chapter 22: The Hamster Era

▼

In Winter 1942, Jan brings Rys a new pet: a hamster. Noting all the animals living in their midst, Antonina calls her house a "Noah's Ark" and declares this the beginning of "the Hamster Era."

This chapter emphasizes how the villa has become a sanctuary for both human refugees and animal companions, reinforcing the parallel between saving human and animal lives.

Key Points:

  • New pet hamster for Rys (Winter 1942)
  • Antonina calls the house "Noah's Ark"
  • "The Hamster Era" begins
  • Villa as sanctuary for both humans and animals
  • Biblical allusion to Noah saving creatures from destruction

Chapter 23: Maurycy Fraenkel Arrives

▼

A new Guest arrives: Maurycy Fraenkel. He is described as pale, sickly, "a husk or a shell" - clearly showing the physical toll of persecution.

Maurycy is a classical musician. To protect him from the housekeeper (who might give him away), Antonina creates a cover story: Maurycy pretends to be Antonina's doctor. This gives him a legitimate reason to be in the house.

In a touching detail, Maurycy soon becomes best friends with the hamster, finding comfort in the small creature.

Key Points:

  • Maurycy Fraenkel - new Guest, classical musician
  • Physical description: pale, sickly, "a husk or a shell"
  • Cover story: pretends to be Antonina's doctor
  • Concern about housekeeper potentially betraying them
  • Maurycy bonds with the hamster - human-animal connection
  • Theme: music and art providing comfort in dark times

Main Characters

A

Antonina Zabinska

The Zookeeper's Wife

The heart of the rescue operation. She manages the daily care of Guests, uses her piano playing as a warning system, and maintains a sense of normalcy in the villa. She has an extraordinary connection with animals and humans alike. She calls the house "Noah's Ark."

J

Jan Zabinski

The Zookeeper

Director of the Warsaw Zoo and active member of the Polish Underground and Zegota. He orchestrates dangerous rescue missions into the Ghetto, including smuggling Lonia Tenenbaum to safety. Bold, resourceful, and willing to take extreme risks.

R

Ryszard "Rys" Zabinski

The Son

Jan and Antonina's young son who grows up surrounded by animals and hidden Guests. He receives a hamster in Chapter 22, loves animals, and considers zoo creatures his personal pets.

M

Magdalena Gross

The Sculptor Friend

Antonina's close friend who arrives at the zoo in Spring 1942. A sculptor who jokes about "sculpting in flour" since she can't work in clay. She helps with daily tasks and experiences the psychological toll of living in hiding.

F

Witold Wroblewski

"The Fox Man"

A Pole who runs the Nazi-approved fur farm at the zoo. His presence provides cover for rescue activities. He has a cat named Balbina who nurses baby foxes. Known for the humorous incident of letting birds eat noodles from his hair.

M

Maurycy Fraenkel

The Musician Guest

A classical musician who arrives pale and sickly, described as "a husk or a shell." He pretends to be Antonina's doctor to avoid suspicion from the housekeeper. He forms a close bond with the hamster.

L

Lonia Tenenbaum

Rescued from the Ghetto

Widow of Dr. Tenenbaum who is smuggled out of the Ghetto by Jan in Chapter 16. She stays at the zoo briefly before moving to the countryside, leaving behind her dog Zarka.

H

Henryk Goldszmit

The Pediatrician/Orphanage Director

Also known as Janusz Korczak. A pediatrician who ran an orphanage. In August 1942, he and all the orphans were sent to Treblinka death camp. An asteroid was named after him in 1971.

Major Themes

Deception as Survival

Chapter 16 opens comparing the Zabinskis' activities to animal survival tactics like chameleons and lion-fish. Throughout these chapters, deception is essential: fake identities (Maurycy as a doctor), warning signals (piano music), and hiding in plain sight (the fur farm). Deception becomes a moral necessity when facing an immoral enemy.

The Zoo as Noah's Ark

Antonina explicitly calls the villa "Noah's Ark" in Chapter 22. Just as Noah saved animals from the flood, the Zabinskis save both animals and humans from Nazi destruction. The parallel extends to the idea of preserving life and diversity against forces of annihilation.

Human-Animal Connections

Animals provide comfort, normalcy, and even cover for the rescue operations. Maurycy bonds with the hamster; Balbina the cat nurses fox cubs; Szczurcio the muskrat entertains. These connections highlight the healing power of nature and the shared vulnerability of all living creatures.

Maintaining Humanity in Inhumane Times

Daily rituals like baking bread, making croissants, and caring for pets maintain a sense of normalcy. Magdalena's joke about sculpting in flour shows how people preserve their identities and spirits. These small acts of humanity resist the dehumanization of Nazi occupation.

Courage and Risk

Every rescue operation risks death for both rescuers and rescued. Jan's bold confrontation with the guard's wife, the decision to harbor Guests despite the danger, and the constant vigilance required all demonstrate extraordinary courage. Helping Jews carried the death penalty.

Art and Music in Crisis

Music serves dual purposes: the piano warning signal and cultural survival. Magdalena (sculptor) and Maurycy (musician) represent artists whose creative lives are disrupted but not destroyed. Art provides both practical function and psychological sustenance.

The Parallel Destruction of Nature and Humanity

Chapter 17 explicitly draws the parallel: what Nazis did to the zoo (killing animals, replacing them with "pure" German specimens) they did to humanity. This theme connects environmentalism with humanism, showing how ideologies of "purity" destroy diversity.
